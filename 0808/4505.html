Matthew,
Basically, the higher the ceiling, the longer the motor needs to be. At the
USIC, where the ceiling is something like 115' high & winning flight times
need to be 9.5 to 10 minutes, we are flying with a 18" long loop because we
need a lot of turns. In lower ceiling sites like a typical gym (20'?) & flight
times of 5 minutes +/-, the motor length is more like 13". Prop blade angle
will have a large influnce on the porp RPM & therefore the rate at which the
turns are used up. As an example, if your average prop RPM is 350, you need
approximately 1750 total turns in the motor at launch for a 5 minute flight. In
low ceilings an additional challenge is avoiding getting hung up on the
joists, etc.
However, all of the above is determined by a lot of testing & careful record
keeping. One of my friends jokes that the hobby isn't building & flying, it
is recording data.
By the way, the method of covering (shrinking & wrinkling) you describe
should work very well.
Good luck.
Gary Hodson

Bill,
That is interesting. First, my reply to Matthew was based on the assumption
that he was flying in a typical gym. In a high ceiling site like the USIC,
we
(Emil Schutzel, Tem Johnson & myself) also use 18" long motors. Our motors
average .85 - .90 grams & our props are 45 or 47 degrees. If we use lower
pitch props we invariably run out of turns.
In low ceiling sites, we still prefer higher pitch props to slow down the
prop & also slow down the climb. In our local contests (low ceilings with
lots
of trusses, etc) a winning time is at least 5 minutes & can be as high as 6
 
minutes.
Gary Hodson

I am not familiar with that particular plan. However, based on my
experience, I think you are on the right track with the information below.
Two
comments. I doubt that you need to increase the rudder area, but doing so
won't hurt
either. I think the 0.030" rubber will prove to be too small. If you are
flying in a typical gym, I would use a 13"-15" loop of 0.034" rubber. The
thicker
rubber will improve the cruise portion of the flight. If you are not happy
with the results, look around for a proven design & build it exactly as
shown.
Don't try to improve it until you have a good base line of experience with
the proven design.
Gary Hodson
